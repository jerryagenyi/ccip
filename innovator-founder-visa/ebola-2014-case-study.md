## Executive Summary

The 2014-2016 Ebola outbreak in West Africa was the deadliest in history, claiming over 11,000 lives across Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone. A critical factor in the outbreak's severity was the failure of crisis communication strategies that did not account for cultural contexts, local beliefs, and semiotic meanings.

**This case study demonstrates how CCIP's Semiotic Intelligence Layer would have transformed the response and saved lives.**

## The Communication Failures

### Failure 1: "Physical Contact" Message

**WHO Message:**
> "Avoid physical contact with infected individuals."

**What Went Wrong:**

| Cultural Context | Meaning | Result |
|------------------|---------|--------|
| West African greeting customs | Touching is social obligation | Message interpreted as "sexual contact," not "touching" |
| Caregiving norms | Caring for sick relatives is moral duty | Families continued touching loved ones |
| Funeral practices | Washing bodies is religious obligation | Unsafe burials continued |

**Result:** People continued risky behaviours because messaging didn't account for cultural semiotics.

---

### Failure 2: Ignoring Trust Networks

**Messengers:** Government officials, international NGOs

**Problem:** In many affected communities:
- Government = mistrusted (corruption, history of neglect)
- International NGOs = viewed with suspicion
- Local elites = seen as disconnected from communities

**Impact:** Messages from "untrusted" sources were ignored or actively resisted.

---

### Failure 3: One-Size-Fits-All Messaging

**Strategy:** English-language, text-based messages, generic content

**Problems:**
- Low literacy (especially in rural areas)
- Language barriers (multiple local languages)
- Generic messaging not tailored to specific communities
- No feedback loops to understand what was working

**Impact:** Information reached people but didn't change behaviour.
